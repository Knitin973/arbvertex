// CONCEPT

What is Futures Arbitrage?

Arbitrage is the practice of profiting from price differences of the same asset across different markets — simultaneously buying low and selling high.

When Bitcoin trades at $68,400 on Binance but $68,750 on Bybit — that $350 gap is an opportunity. An arbitrageur buys on the cheaper exchange and simultaneously sells on the expensive one, locking in profit before the gap closes.

Futures arbitrage adds another layer: the difference between a coin's spot price and its futures contract price — called the "basis." This basis is predictable, measurable, and exploitable.

The key insight: you don't need to predict where the market goes. You only need a price gap to exist — and you profit when it closes.

// Spot vs Futures

Buy on spot market, short the futures contract. When both prices converge at expiry, you collect the spread as profit — regardless of market direction.

// Cross-Exchange

Same asset, two exchanges, different prices. Detect the gap, execute both legs simultaneously, close for guaranteed spread profit.

// Funding Rate

Perpetual futures pay or charge funding every 8 hours. When funding is high, hold spot and short the perp — collect funding as passive income.

// RISK WARNING

Risks & Precautions

Arbitrage is lower risk than directional trading — but it is NOT risk-free. Read this carefully before you start.

⚠️

Funding Rate Warning: The funding rate can flip negative at any time. If you are holding a long perp position and funding turns negative — you pay instead of receive. This can turn a profitable setup into a loss within 8 hours. Always monitor funding before entering a position.

🛡️ PRECAUTIONS BEFORE TAKING A TRADE
✓

Never put all funds in a single trade. Split your capital across multiple positions — if one trade goes wrong, your entire account isn't at risk.

✓

Enter in parts (DCA into position). Don't go full size at once. Enter in 2–3 tranches to average your price and reduce entry risk, especially in volatile markets.

✓

Always keep sufficient funds on both exchanges. You need margin on both the buy leg and sell leg. Underfunding either side will leave you with unhedged directional exposure — a serious risk.

HIGH RISK

Negative Funding Rate Reversal

Funding rates change every 8 hours. If you entered expecting positive funding and it reverses to negative, you lose money on the funding leg. Exit immediately when funding drops near zero.

HIGH RISK

Execution Risk (Partial Fills)

If only one leg of your trade fills (buy without sell, or vice versa), you have unhedged directional exposure. A fast market move can cause significant loss. Always use limit orders and verify both fills.

MEDIUM RISK

Slippage & Trading Fees

On low-liquidity pairs, you may get filled at a worse price than expected. Combined with taker fees (0.05–0.1%) on both legs, small spreads can disappear entirely. Only trade when net spread > 0.25%.

MEDIUM RISK

Exchange Risk

Exchanges can freeze withdrawals, get hacked, or become insolvent (see: FTX 2022). Never keep more capital on an exchange than you need for active trades. Distribute across 2–3 exchanges.

MEDIUM RISK

Spread Compression

When many traders exploit the same opportunity, the spread shrinks. A signal that showed 0.5% profit may only yield 0.1% by the time you execute. Speed matters — delayed execution kills profit.

LOW RISK

Regulatory & Tax Risk

Crypto regulations vary by country. Profits from arbitrage may be taxable. Consult a local tax advisor. Some exchanges may restrict access based on your country of residence.

What is Crypto Arbitrage? Complete Beginner Guide 2026

Crypto arbitrage means buying an asset cheaply on one exchange and selling it higher on another — instantly locking in profit. No price prediction required.

Simple Definition

Arbitrage is profiting from the same asset being priced differently in two places at the same time. In crypto, this happens constantly — Binance may price BTC at $68,200 while Bybit prices it at $68,600. That $400 gap = profit.

Why Does the Gap Exist?

Different exchanges have different buyers and sellers. Order flow, liquidity, and regional demand create temporary price differences. These gaps close quickly — that's why speed matters.

3 Types of Crypto Arbitrage

1. Cross-Exchange Arb

Buy on exchange A, sell on exchange B. Profit = price gap minus fees.

2. Funding Rate Arb

Earn passive income from perpetual futures every 8 hours — zero directional risk.

3. Triangular Arb

Exploit price inefficiency between 3 trading pairs on the same exchange.

Crypto Arbitrage Risks You Must Know Before Starting in 2026

Arbitrage is low-risk — not zero risk. Know these before putting in real capital.

⚠️ Slippage

Large orders move the market before both legs fill. Stick to signals with 2%+ gap to absorb slippage.

⚠️ Execution Lag

Manual execution is slow. A 0.3% gap can vanish in 2 seconds. Use limit orders pre-set, or rely on ArbVertex entry windows.

⚠️ Funding Rate Reversal

Positive funding can turn negative overnight. Always exit when funding drops below +0.01%.

⚠️ Exchange Risk

Keep only what you need on each exchange. Don't leave large idle balances.

⚠️ Margin Liquidation

In futures arb, keep 2–3× the minimum margin to avoid forced liquidation.

Arbitrage Trading Fees Explained: Maker, Taker, Withdrawal & Slippage Costs in 2026

Most beginners underestimate fees and lose money on arbitrage. Know all 4 cost types before your first trade.

1. Taker Fee

Paid when you fill someone else's order (market order). Binance: 0.04%, Bybit: 0.055%, MEXC: 0.01%

2. Maker Fee

Lower fee if you place a limit order and wait. Bybit maker: 0.01%. Use limit orders to save 0.03–0.05%.

3. Withdrawal Fee

Charged when moving coins between exchanges. Use USDT on TRC-20 (1 USDT fee) instead of ERC-20 ($5–15 fee).

4. Slippage

Large order moves market before filling. Stick to BTC/ETH for lowest slippage. Avoid low-volume altcoins unless gap is 1%+.
