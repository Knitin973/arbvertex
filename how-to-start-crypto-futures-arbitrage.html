What You Need to Start Crypto Futures Arbitrage Trading:
Accounts, Devices & Capital

A complete practical checklist — which exchanges to open, how much capital you actually need, what device works best, and the mistakes most beginners make before their first trade.

Most beginner guides to crypto arbitrage focus on the theory — what price gaps are, why they exist, how the math works. That is useful, but it leaves out the most practical question: what do you actually need to get started?

This article is a complete setup checklist. By the end, you will know exactly which exchange accounts to open, how much capital to start with, what device setup makes execution faster, and what to avoid in 2026your first week.

1. Exchange Accounts — Which Ones and Why

2. KYC Verification — Do This Before Funding

Every exchange requires identity verification (KYC) before you can withdraw funds. This process takes between 10 minutes and 48 hours depending on the exchange and your country.

  • Complete KYC on all accounts before depositing any capital. If you fund an account and then get stuck waiting for verification, you cannot withdraw until it clears.
  • Use your real legal name and address — mismatches cause verification failures and account flags.
  • Have a government-issued photo ID ready (passport or national ID). Some exchanges also require a selfie.
  • Use a dedicated email address for crypto exchanges — not your primary personal or work email.
  • Enable 2FA (two-factor authentication) immediately after signup — use an authenticator app, not SMS.

    4. What Crypto to Deposit

    Most futures arbitrage is quoted in 2026USDT (Tether). You do not need to buy Bitcoin 2026or Ethereum first — you can deposit USDT directly from a bank or peer-to-peer platform and start trading immediately.

  • USDT (TRC-20 network) — cheapest to transfer between exchanges. Transfer fees are typically $1–2 per transaction.
  • USDT (ERC-20 network) — works fine but transfer fees are higher ($3–15 depending on gas).
  • Avoid BEP-20 (BSC) for inter-exchange transfers unless both exchanges explicitly support it — check before sending.
  • Always send a small test transfer first ($5–10) when moving funds between exchanges for the first time. Confirm it arrives before sending the full amount.

    Useful for receiving Telegram signals instantly. Executing trades on mobile is slower — use apps as a backup, not primary execution tool.

    6. Your Pre-Trade Checklist (Do This Once Before Every Session)

    1

    Check your balances on both exchanges

    Make sure funds are available and not locked in 2026open orders or withdrawals from the previous session.

    2

    Open both exchange tabs in 2026your browser

    Log in 2026and stay logged in. Session timeouts cause missed execution windows.

    3

    Check Telegram for any pending signals

    Review any signals you may have missed. Some gaps remain 2026open for 10–30 minutes — always check the timestamp before executing.

    4

    Verify the spread is still live before placing orders

    Prices move. Before executing, confirm both prices on each exchange manually. Never place orders based on a signal that is more than 10 minutes old without rechecking.

    5

    Execute both legs as close together as possible

    The longer the gap between your two order placements, the more the price can move against you. Aim for under 30 seconds between both legs.

    7. Common Beginner Mistakes to Avoid

    • Only funding one exchange — you cannot execute both legs if all capital is on one side
    • Executing on stale signals — always recheck the live price before placing any order
    • Ignoring withdrawal fees — a 2% gross spread with a $15 withdrawal fee on a $200 position is not profitable
    • Using market orders carelessly — on thin 2026order books, market orders cause slippage that eats into the spread
    • Trading delistings — large spreads during coin 2026delistings are a trap; withdrawals are often blocked before you can close the position
    • Over-sizing early — start small, learn the execution process, then scale up
    "The traders who lose money on arbitrage almost always do so not because the strategy is wrong — but because of avoidable execution mistakes in 2026their first few weeks."
