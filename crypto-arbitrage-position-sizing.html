Position Sizing for Crypto Arbitrage: A Practical Guide

Learn how to size arbitrage positions correctly — Kelly Criterion, risk percentage methods, and practical frameworks for retail arbitrage traders.

Why Position Sizing Matters

The difference between a profitable and unprofitable arbitrage trader often isn't the strategy — it's position sizing.

Too small: Returns are insignificant.

Too large: One bad period wipes out months of gains..

The Kelly Criterion

Kelly formula: f = (bp - q) / b.

For arbitrage with 70% win rate, 2:1 reward/risk: f = 55% Kelly.

Full Kelly is too aggressive — most practitioners use 1/4 to 1/2 Kelly: 14-28% per position..

Practical Risk Percentage Method

Simpler alternative: Risk 1-2% of total portfolio per trade.

Calculate position size: Position = (Portfolio × Risk%) / (Entry - Stop Loss).

Cap at 10% of portfolio maximum..

Funding Rate Arb Specific Sizing

For hedged funding rate positions (near-zero market risk): Larger position sizes are acceptable.

20-50% of portfolio per position is reasonable.

Spread across 3-5 positions (10-15% each).

Maximum single exchange exposure: 40-50% of total portfolio..

Scaling Positions

Start small (5-10% of capital per position) even if your framework suggests more.

Learn how the position behaves in practice.

After 10+ successful trades with the sizing, increase to target levels..

Dynamic Sizing

Increase position size when: Funding rate is high (0.1%+), market regime is bull, you've had 5+ consecutive profitable trades.

Decrease when: Rates are marginal, market is bear or volatile, you've had 2+ losing trades recently..

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I use the same position size for every trade?
Not necessarily. Higher-quality signals warrant larger positions. Lower-quality signals warrant smaller.
How do I size a cross-exchange trade?
Position = Amount you can execute on both exchanges within the spread window without moving the market.
What if my position sizing feels too conservative?
It probably isn't. The biggest risk in trading isn't missing gains — it's blowing up capital.
