Position Sizing for Crypto Arbitrage: A Practical Guide

The difference between a profitable and unprofitable arbitrage trader often is not the strategy — it is position sizing. Too small and returns are insignificant. Too large and one bad period wipes out months of gains. This guide gives you exact frameworks with real numbers.

📌Why Position Sizing Matters

Most beginners focus entirely on finding good signals — and ignore how much capital to deploy per trade. This is backwards. Even a perfectly identified arbitrage opportunity can result in net losses if the position is too large relative to fees, slippage, or margin requirements.

Consider two traders with the same $10,000 portfolio and the same 70% win rate signals. Trader A risks 5% per trade. Trader B risks 25% per trade. After 20 trades with identical signal quality, Trader A compounds steadily. Trader B may be wiped out by a single bad streak — even though their signals were correct 70% of the time.

In arbitrage specifically, position sizing also determines your exchange exposure risk, margin buffer, and liquidity risk — all of which are separate from price risk.

📐The Kelly Criterion

The Kelly Criterion is a mathematical formula that calculates the optimal fraction of your portfolio to risk on any bet or trade, given a known win rate and reward-to-risk ratio.

Kelly Formula f* = (b × p − q) / b where: b = reward/risk ratio · p = win probability · q = loss probability (1−p) Example — Arbitrage signal: 70% win rate, 2:1 reward/risk f* = (2 × 0.70 − 0.30) / 2 f* = (1.40 − 0.30) / 2 f* = 0.55 → 55% of portfolio Half Kelly (recommended) Full Kelly (55%) is dangerously aggressive in practice. Most professionals use ¼ to ½ Kelly → 14% to 28% per position

Kelly assumes a fixed win rate and reward ratio — which arbitrage does not always have cleanly. Use it as an upper bound, not a target. For most retail traders, Half Kelly (25–28%) is the absolute maximum for a single position.

🎯Risk Percentage Method

The simpler and more practical alternative to Kelly. Risk a fixed percentage of your total portfolio on each trade — regardless of signal strength. This keeps drawdowns predictable and prevents a single loss from causing serious damage.

Formula Position Size = (Portfolio × Risk%) ÷ Max Loss per Unit Example — $10,000 portfolio, 2% risk rule, 1% stop loss Risk amount: $10,000 × 2% = $200 Position size: $200 ÷ 1% = $20,000 notional (using 2× leverage — or $10,000 at 1× with 2% stop) Hard cap rule Never exceed 10% of portfolio on a single directional trade. For delta-neutral arb positions, cap raises to 20–30% per position.

The risk % method is beginner-friendly because it scales automatically. As your portfolio grows, position sizes grow proportionally — you never need to recalculate manually.

💰Funding Rate Arb Specific Sizing

Funding rate arbitrage is delta-neutral — meaning your net market price exposure is near zero. This changes the sizing math significantly. Because you are not exposed to price direction, larger positions are acceptable compared to directional trades.

However, you are still exposed to: funding rate reversal, exchange risk, and margin liquidation. These risks set the sizing ceiling.

Recommended portfolio allocation — Funding Rate Arb Total arb allocation: 60–80% of portfolio Per position max: 20–25% of portfolio Number of positions: 3–5 simultaneous Single exchange max: 40% of total portfolio Margin buffer per pos: 2–3× minimum requirement Example — $10,000 portfolio, 4 positions ETH/USDT: $2,500 (25%) — Binance spot + Bybit short BTC/USDT: $2,500 (25%) — Binance spot + Bybit short SOL/USDT: $1,500 (15%) — Gate spot + Bybit short BNB/USDT: $1,500 (15%) — Binance spot + Binance short Reserve: $2,000 (20%) — Margin buffer + opportunity fund

The 20% reserve is not idle capital — it serves as your margin top-up fund in case of price spikes against your short leg, and as dry powder for new opportunities when a position is closed.

📈Scaling Roadmap

Even if your framework says 20% per position from day one — start smaller. Real execution has surprises that paper trading does not. Scale up only after your results confirm the framework is working in practice.

Phase 1 — Beginner
Trades 1–10

  • → 5% per position max
  • → 1–2 positions only
  • → ETH or BTC only
  • → 40% reserve held
  • → Journal every trade

Phase 2 — Intermediate
Trades 10–30

  • → 10–15% per position
  • → 2–3 positions
  • → Add 1 altcoin pair
  • → 25% reserve held
  • → Track slippage

Phase 3 — Advanced
30+ trades

  • → 20–25% per position
  • → 3–5 positions
  • → Full pair diversity
  • → 20% reserve held
  • → Dynamic sizing active

The phase progression is trade-count based — not time based. A trader who completes 10 trades in 2 weeks moves to Phase 2 faster than one who takes 3 months. What matters is completed trade experience, not time spent.

⚡Dynamic Sizing Rules

Once in Phase 3, you can actively adjust position sizes based on current market conditions. These rules help you press when conditions are favourable and pull back when risk is elevated.

✅ Increase Size When

  • → Funding rate ≥ 0.08%/8h
  • → Rate positive 10+ consecutive payments
  • → OI rising (bull market)
  • → 5+ consecutive profitable trades
  • → Spread is 2%+ above your cost

⚠️ Decrease Size When

  • → Funding rate below 0.03%/8h
  • → Rate trending down 3+ payments
  • → OI falling (bear pressure)
  • → 2+ consecutive losing trades
  • → Major news event expected

Dynamic sizing should only scale within your phase limits — not beyond them. If you are in Phase 2 (15% max), dynamic sizing means varying between 8–15%, not exceeding 15%. The phase ceiling is a hard cap regardless of market conditions.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

Should I use the same position size for every trade?
Not in Phase 3 — but yes in Phase 1 and 2. Consistency early on removes one variable from your analysis. Once you have 30+ trades of data, you can start varying sizes based on signal quality and market conditions.
How do I size a cross-exchange spot arb trade?
Position = the maximum amount you can execute on both exchanges within the spread window without moving the market. For BTC/ETH this is typically $10K–$50K. For altcoins, check order book depth — your order should not exceed 0.5% of the visible ask volume.
What if my position sizing feels too conservative?
It probably is not. The biggest risk in trading is not missing gains — it is permanent capital loss. A 30% drawdown requires a 43% gain just to break even. Conservative sizing protects your ability to stay in the game long enough to compound.
Can I use leverage to increase position size?
No — not on the futures leg of a funding rate arb trade. Leverage on the short leg destroys the delta-neutral hedge. A 10% price spike with 5× leverage liquidates your short before the spot leg compensates. Use 1× only. If you want larger returns, increase capital — not leverage.
How does exchange risk affect sizing?
Single exchange exposure should never exceed 40% of your total portfolio — even across multiple positions on that exchange. If Binance goes down with 60% of your capital, you are stuck. Distribute across at least 2 exchanges at all times.
When should I increase my total portfolio allocation to arb?
Only after completing Phase 2 (10–30 trades) with positive results. Start at 40–50% of your total crypto portfolio in arb positions. Move to 60–80% only in Phase 3 with 30+ completed trades and a consistent positive P&L journal.
